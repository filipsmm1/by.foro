Taste Is Built, Not Found

Taste is often described as instinct, but instinct becomes sharper through attention, comparison and return.

by.foro Editorial19 July 20266 min read

In this story

  1. Looking is an active practice
  2. Build a reference library
  3. Follow influence sideways
  4. Delay the verdict

Taste is often described as instinct, but instinct becomes sharper through attention, comparison and return.

Looking is an active practice

Seeing many images is not the same as looking. Looking asks what creates the response: scale, rhythm, material, framing, tension, context or memory. Without that description, preference remains vague and easy to manipulate.

The useful question is not only whether something is liked. It is what the object is doing and whether it does that convincingly. This creates space to admire work that is not personally desirable and to criticise work that is immediately attractive.

Build a reference library

A reference library can be physical, digital or both. Its value depends less on size than on the quality of the notes attached to it. Save the exact page, scene, garment, room or sentence, then record what made it worth keeping.

Return later. Novelty produces false intensity. If the reference still feels alive after distance, it probably contains something deeper than the mood of a single day.

Follow influence sideways

Interesting discoveries often sit beside the obvious object. Read the credits. Look at the set designer, editor, typographer, producer, architect or book mentioned in an interview. A single work can become a network of names and earlier decisions.

This sideways movement makes taste less dependent on feeds. It replaces recommendation with research and allows connections to form across categories that platforms usually keep separate.

Delay the verdict

Immediate certainty is efficient but visually limiting. Some of the most useful work is initially difficult, awkward or resistant. Delaying the verdict does not require pretending to like it. It means staying long enough to understand the terms of the work.

Taste becomes specific when preference is supported by language and memory. It is not found as a finished identity. It is built through repeated attention and the willingness to revise.
